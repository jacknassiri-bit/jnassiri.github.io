How I research and write

Every post on this blog starts with a question I genuinely do not know the answer to. Here is how I go from curiosity to a finished article.

01

Finding a topic worth writing about

I follow medical and AI news closely, mostly through STAT News, Nature, and my school library's access to journal databases. My dad is a doctor, so I can ask him directly when something in a study does not make sense. Those conversations help me figure out what is actually clinically relevant versus what just sounds impressive on paper. A topic earns a spot on my list when two things are true: there is real primary research behind it, and the public understanding of that research is either missing or wrong.

I also pay attention to what confuses me. If I read a headline and realize I cannot explain the underlying mechanism, that is usually a sign the article is worth writing. If I am confused, someone else probably is too.

02

Reading the primary source first

Before I read any news coverage of a study, I try to find and read the original paper. I use PubMed, Google Scholar, and bioRxiv for this. Reading the abstract and methods section tells me things that journalism almost always leaves out: sample size, study design, limitations, and whether the effect size is actually meaningful.

If I cannot access the full paper, I use the NIH's PubMed Central database, which has free access to a large portion of federally funded research.

Where I find papers

  • PubMed
  • Google Scholar
  • bioRxiv / medRxiv
  • NIH PubMed Central
  • Semantic Scholar
03

Checking the claim against other sources

A single study is not a fact. I look for whether the finding has been replicated, whether it has been covered by credible medical journalists, and whether researchers in the relevant field have commented on it publicly. If a result is brand new and has not been independently replicated, I say so in the article.

I also look for expert pushback. The comment sections of papers on PubPeer, responses published in the same journal, and expert interviews cited in STAT News or the Lancet often reveal limitations that the original authors downplayed.

04

Writing for a general audience without losing accuracy

The hardest part of this kind of writing is finding analogies that are accurate enough to be honest without being so simplified they mislead. I try to explain the mechanism first, then the implications. I avoid passive hedging words like "may suggest" unless the evidence genuinely only suggests rather than demonstrates something.

I write a draft, then read it back and ask: would a smart person with no science background understand this? Would a medical professional find anything here that is wrong? Both questions matter.

05

Adding citations and publishing

Every factual claim in a finished article links to its source. I include the citation at the bottom of each post so readers can verify anything I wrote, read further, or catch me if I got something wrong. Getting things right matters more to me than getting things published quickly.

I publish on a weekly schedule, alternating between AI in Medicine and Med Tech topics. If I am not confident enough in a draft, I hold it rather than rush it out.
